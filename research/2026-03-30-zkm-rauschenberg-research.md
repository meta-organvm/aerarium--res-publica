# ZKM Rauschenberg Residencies 2026: Comprehensive Application Requirements and Program Analysis

The ZKM Rauschenberg Residencies represent a significant international artistic research opportunity launched in collaboration between the ZKM | Center for Art and Media Karlsruhe and the Robert Rauschenberg Foundation to commemorate the centennial of artist Robert Rauschenberg's birth in 2025 and the fifty-year anniversary of the landmark 1966 "9 Evenings: Theater and Engineering" performance series[1][3]. As of March 30, 2026, the application deadline of April 12, 2026 remains active, making this an immediate and critical opportunity for artists working at the intersection of performative art and creative technology[5][5]. The program offers three international residencies providing comprehensive support including a €4,000 scholarship, €1,000 travel subsidy, accommodation, and up to €2,500 for material costs, coupled with access to state-of-the-art technical facilities and the archives of Experiments in Art and Technology (E.A.T.) housed at ZKM since 1999[1][3][3]. This report provides a structured analysis of all application requirements, funding mechanisms, programmatic expectations, and the institutional relationships undergirding this significant opportunity for contemporary artistic research and development.

## Historical Context and Institutional Background: The Legacy of E.A.T. and the Rauschenberg-ZKM Partnership

### Robert Rauschenberg's Revolutionary Vision and the Experiments in Art and Technology Movement

Robert Rauschenberg, who would have celebrated his hundredth birthday in October 2025, fundamentally transformed the landscape of contemporary artistic practice through his unwavering commitment to breaking down disciplinary barriers and fostering unprecedented collaboration between artists and technologists[1][1]. In 1966, alongside Bell Laboratories engineer Billy Klüver and artist Robert Whitman, Rauschenberg co-founded Experiments in Art and Technology (E.A.T.), a nonprofit organization whose foundational mission was to facilitate meaningful connections between artists, engineers, and scientists[16][21]. This pioneering initiative emerged directly from the revolutionary "9 Evenings: Theater and Engineering" performance series, which took place at the Armory Hall in New York City from October 13-23, 1966[1][1][1]. The performances represented the first large-scale collaboration between artists and engineers working in concert, with the two groups collaborating for ten months to develop technical equipment and systems that became integral components of the artistic works themselves rather than mere technical support[38]. This foundational commitment to genuine collaboration—where technology served the artistic vision rather than dominating it—established the philosophical cornerstone upon which the current Rauschenberg Residencies are constructed.

E.A.T.'s significance extended far beyond its initial performances through the organization's commitment to what it termed "Projects Outside Art," an ambitious 1969 initiative that reflected E.A.T.'s idealistic vision of the artist as an active partner in broader social projects addressing urban ecology, community connectivity through technology, and play as a social practice[32][32][37]. This framework demonstrated that artistic-technological collaboration could directly address socio-political concerns and contribute to meaningful community transformation. The organization established a comprehensive archive comprising approximately 360 documents including reports, catalogs, newsletters, informational bulletins, proposals, lectures, photographs, notebooks, videos, posters, and newspaper clippings documenting the organization's initial phase of activity[35]. The significance of this archival material is that it remains accessible to contemporary researchers and artists, making the historical foundation of E.A.T.'s work directly relevant to current artistic practice and innovation.

### The ZKM's Commitment to Technology and Media Art

The ZKM | Center for Art and Media Karlsruhe has, since its founding in 1989, embraced a deliberate and forward-looking approach to technology and media art that positions technological developments and socio-political issues as fundamentally interconnected rather than as separate domains[1][3][1][13]. This institutional philosophy distinguishes ZKM from institutions that treat technology as merely a tool; instead, ZKM conceptualizes technology as a critical lens through which contemporary culture, society, and artistic practice must be understood and interrogated. The ZKM has hosted over 600 guest artists and scientists since its establishment, demonstrating a sustained commitment to facilitating artistic-scientific collaboration and providing technical, organizational, and financial support at all levels[13][13]. This extensive history of artist residencies and collaborative projects establishes ZKM as an institution with demonstrated expertise in supporting experimental artistic research across multiple disciplines.

The ZKM | Hertzlab, in particular, operates as a transdisciplinary research and development platform specifically positioned at the interface of media arts, science, and society[20][20]. Named in honor of scientist Heinrich Hertz, who proved the existence of electromagnetic waves through famous spark experiments at the University of Karlsruhe in 1886, the Hertzlab represents the institutional commitment to bridging scientific inquiry and artistic creativity[20]. The Hertzlab's structure encompasses artistic-technological research, curation and supervision of a global residency program, as well as concerts, showcases, and performances of artistic research conducted elsewhere[20]. Established in 2017 through the unification of the former Institute for Visual Media and the Institute for Music and Acoustics, the Hertzlab provides researchers with access to multiple specialized facilities including studios, sound studios, a wet laboratory, a virtual reality laboratory, and co-working spaces[20][1].

### The Institutional Partnership: ZKM and the Robert Rauschenberg Foundation

The collaboration between ZKM and the Robert Rauschenberg Foundation represents a strategic alignment of institutional missions focused on supporting experimental artistic practice and advancing Rauschenberg's legacy of socially engaged art-technology collaboration[1][3][1]. The Robert Rauschenberg Foundation, which builds upon the artistic legacy of Robert Rauschenberg, emphasizes his foundational belief that artists can serve as agents of social change[19][36]. In 2025, the Foundation announced its celebration of Rauschenberg's centennial, with activities extending throughout 2025 and into 2026 designed to revisit Rauschenberg's work through contemporary perspectives while highlighting his prescience and enduring influence on generations of artists and advocates for social progress[34][32].

The Foundation's support for the ZKM Rauschenberg Residencies demonstrates a deliberate strategy to extend Rauschenberg's legacy beyond archival preservation into active, contemporary artistic practice. Rather than treating E.A.T.'s historical achievements as completed historical moments, the partnership situates those principles as continuing frameworks for current artistic inquiry and experimentation. The Foundation has invested in establishing these residencies as part of its broader centennial programming, which includes seven major institutional presentations of Rauschenberg's work internationally, as well as targeted grants supporting conservation, exhibitions, performances, publications, and public programs globally[34]. The ZKM Rauschenberg Residencies thus function as a critical component of a larger international network of cultural institutions engaged in contemporary dialogue with Rauschenberg's artistic philosophy and practice.

## Detailed Application Requirements and Eligibility Criteria

### Eligibility and Required Qualifications

The ZKM Rauschenberg Residencies are specifically designed for artists working at the intersection of performative art and creative technology, representing a deliberately narrow focus that distinguishes this program from more broadly conceived residency opportunities[1][3][3][3]. Eligible applicants must demonstrate professional engagement with performance-based artistic practices that meaningfully incorporate technological elements, whether through digital interfaces, interactive systems, projection technologies, or other technology-mediated approaches[3][33]. The program's emphasis on the "intersection" of these disciplines suggests that superficial technological engagement will not suffice; rather, the technology must be deeply integrated into the conceptual and formal articulation of the performed work.

While the program welcomes team applications in principle, ZKM will not provide additional accommodation or travel cost coverage beyond what is allocated for a single individual[33]. This policy reflects institutional resource constraints while maintaining openness to collaborative submissions. Teams considering applications should understand that shared funding will not increase proportionally with the number of team members; thus, teams must accept the same €4,000 scholarship and €1,000 travel subsidy that individual applicants receive. This structure may actually incentivize individual applications or require team members to negotiate cost-sharing arrangements independently.

A critical eligibility requirement mandates that applicants must possess valid health insurance or international health insurance for Germany at the start of the residency[1][1]. This requirement ensures that all residents maintain appropriate health coverage throughout their three-month stay in Karlsruhe, protecting both individual residents and the institution from complications arising from medical emergencies or health-related issues. Applicants from outside the European Union should investigate whether their existing health insurance provides coverage in Germany or whether they must obtain supplementary international health insurance. Some countries have reciprocal healthcare agreements with Germany, while others may require private insurance purchase. ZKM can assist applicants in obtaining letters of invitation necessary for visa applications, though applicants remain strictly responsible for all visa-related costs[33].

### Portfolio and Application Material Specifications

Applicants must submit a portfolio of relevant digital theater works or interactive performative works utilizing technology from the preceding five years[1][33]. This requirement establishes a clear temporal window demonstrating recent artistic activity and contemporary engagement with technology and performance. The five-year lookback period suggests that the program prioritizes current artistic practice over historical documentation; artists whose most recent substantial work dates beyond five years may face challenges in meeting this requirement unless they can demonstrate ongoing engagement with performance-based work incorporating technology. The portfolio should provide substantial evidence of the applicant's technical competence, artistic vision, and ability to integrate technology meaningfully into performative contexts.

Portfolio materials should be organized and presented in formats readily accessible through standard digital viewing applications. High-quality documentation is essential, as the evaluation committee will assess the applicant's artistic practice primarily through portfolio materials. Video documentation of past performances constitutes particularly effective portfolio evidence, as it allows evaluators to understand the temporal, spatial, and interactive dimensions of the work that still images or text descriptions cannot fully convey. Artists should ensure that video materials are properly compressed for file transfer while maintaining sufficient resolution to communicate the work effectively.

### Project Description and Concept Proposal

The critical component of the application requires a detailed project description limited to a maximum of two DIN A4 pages of text, within which applicants must articulate the content, technological approach, schedule, and a brief cost plan for their proposed residency project[33]. This concise format demands that applicants distill their conceptual vision, methodological approach, and practical execution strategy into densely written, carefully organized prose. DIN A4 paper corresponds to standard European paper dimensions slightly taller and narrower than US letter-size paper; applicants should ensure their document specifications conform to this requirement.

Within this space-constrained format, applicants must address multiple interconnected dimensions of their proposed work. The "content" dimension requires clear articulation of the conceptual framework, thematic concerns, and artistic questions the residency project will investigate. What social, political, aesthetic, or technical problems does the proposed work address? How does the project advance contemporary artistic discourse or practice? The "technological approach" section must explain the specific technologies, platforms, or systems the artist intends to employ, while critically addressing why these particular technological choices best serve the conceptual and artistic objectives. ZKM | Hertzlab evaluators have explicitly stated that they are "less interested in tech buzzwords than in a compelling connection between the theme and the selected technology and in the creative use of the selected method"[3][3][3][3]. This statement suggests that applications featuring trendy technological terminology without substantive conceptual grounding will be viewed skeptically. Instead, applicants should demonstrate thoughtful engagement with how particular technologies enable, constrain, or transform their artistic vision.

The "schedule" component requires applicants to propose a realistic timeline for their three-month residency, breaking down the project into phases of research, development, experimentation, technical troubleshooting, and final presentation preparation. This demonstrates project management competence and acknowledgment of the practical realities involved in producing complex technologically-mediated performance work. Finally, the "brief cost plan" section asks applicants to estimate material and production costs, providing justification for how the available €2,500 material cost allocation will be deployed. Realistic cost estimation demonstrates professional competence and practical understanding of production budgets.

## Comprehensive Funding and Support Parameters

### Financial Support and Accommodation Provisions

The ZKM Rauschenberg Residencies provide multifaceted financial and material support enabling selected residents to focus on artistic development without the distraction of accommodation or subsistence costs. The program offers a €4,000 scholarship providing core financial support for the three-month residency period[1][3][3][3][18][3][3]. This substantial sum, while modest by some international residency standards, represents meaningful institutional investment in resident support. Calculated across the twelve-week residency, this scholarship averages approximately €333 per week, sufficient to supplement accommodation costs and provide modest subsistence support when combined with provided housing.

In addition to the core scholarship, the program provides a €1,000 subsidy specifically designated for travel expenses[1][3][3][3][18][3][3]. This allocation recognizes that residents traveling from distant geographic locations face substantial travel costs; the €1,000 amount partially offsets these expenses for residents traveling from Europe or moderately distant international locations, though residents from distant regions such as Asia, Australia, or the Americas may find that this allocation covers only a portion of their actual travel costs. Residents should budget for the possibility that their actual travel expenses may exceed this allocation.

The program additionally offers up to €2,500 designated specifically for material costs directly related to the residency project[1][3][3][3][18][3][3]. This amount provides substantial resources for acquiring specialized materials, electronics, sensors, projection equipment, audio components, or other technological elements essential to the resident's project. The specification that these funds are available "up to" €2,500 suggests that this represents the maximum available allocation; residents should not presume automatic access to the full amount but rather justify specific material needs within their cost plan. Materials purchased during the residency likely remain the property of the artist unless specific agreements regarding institutional ownership are negotiated.

### Housing and Accommodation Arrangements

The program includes accommodation in a ZKM artist share apartment, a significant benefit that eliminates housing search complexity and provides community among residents[1][3][3][3][18][3][3]. While the program documentation does not specify apartment specifications or amenities, the designation as a "share apartment" indicates shared living arrangements rather than private accommodation, reflecting both cost-efficiency and the program's emphasis on community and collaboration. Residents should anticipate dormitory-style or shared residential arrangements rather than private apartments. This communal structure potentially facilitates inter-resident collaboration and creates informal networks among the three selected residents and other ZKM-associated artists.

The housing arrangement represents substantial institutional support, as contemporary Karlsruhe rental costs for a private apartment would likely consume a significant portion of the modest €4,000 scholarship. By providing housing, ZKM ensures that residents can allocate financial support toward project materials and subsistence expenses rather than housing costs. The provision of shared housing also reflects the program's philosophical alignment with Rauschenberg's commitment to community-based artistic practice, wherein shared physical spaces facilitate creative exchange and collaborative potential.

### Technical Infrastructure and Workspace Access

Beyond financial support, the program provides residents with access to ZKM's comprehensive technical infrastructure, a resource whose value far exceeds the direct financial allocations. The specifications indicate that residents will have on-site access to studios, sound studios, wet laboratory facilities, VR laboratory facilities, and co-working space[1][33]. This constellation of specialized environments enables artistic work spanning diverse technological and formal approaches. The "wet laboratory" likely refers to spaces equipped for physical experimentation, prototyping, and materials testing—environments supporting analog and hybrid artistic practices. The VR laboratory provides access to virtual reality development tools and systems supporting immersive performance and interactive environment creation. Sound studios enable acoustic experimentation, audio processing, recording, and spatialized sound design essential to contemporary performance work.

The provision of diverse workspace types recognizes that contemporary performative art incorporating technology encompasses highly varied methodological approaches and technical requirements. Artists developing interactive dance works incorporating motion capture require different technical infrastructure than those exploring sonically-generated visual environments or physically-responsive architectural installations. The breadth of available facilities indicates that ZKM anticipates this diversity and has designed residency infrastructure to accommodate multiple artistic approaches simultaneously.

### Mentoring, Support, and Institutional Integration

The program explicitly includes "mentoring and support" as core program components, though the documentation provides limited detail regarding the specific nature, frequency, or structure of mentoring relationships[1][3][3][3][18][3][3]. This vagueness regarding mentorship deserves clarification during the application inquiry stage; interested artists should contact ZKM directly to understand what mentoring arrangements entail, whether mentoring relationships pair residents with specific senior artists or ZKM staff, and what expectations regarding mentorship frequency and scope exist. In many contemporary residency programs, mentoring relationships prove crucial to resident development, providing critical feedback, facilitating institutional network connections, and offering guidance regarding technical problem-solving.

The program's emphasis on support extends beyond mentoring to encompass the broader institutional infrastructure of ZKM, including curatorial guidance, technical assistance from ZKM's engineering and technical staff, and integration into ZKM's artistic community. The promise of institutional support suggests that residents should expect responsive assistance with technical challenges, facility access coordination, and problem-solving regarding their specific projects.

### Access to the Experiments in Art and Technology Archive

A particularly significant though under-emphasized program benefit involves access to the Experiments in Art and Technology archive, continuously housed at ZKM since 1999[7][32][32][35]. This archive comprises the most comprehensive collection of E.A.T. documentation available globally, containing correspondence, organizational records, project documentation, photographic materials, and other primary source materials documenting the organization's history and philosophical development[35]. For residents engaged with performance art and technology, this archive provides direct access to the historical precedent and conceptual foundation underlying their contemporary practice. Artists can examine how E.A.T. conceptualized artist-engineer collaboration, how specific technical innovations emerged from artistic needs, and how E.A.T. navigated the institutional, social, and creative challenges of interdisciplinary practice.

The archive access reflects the program's framing of contemporary artistic research not as disconnected from historical precedent but rather as continuation, dialogue, and transformation of established artistic-technological traditions. Residents can position their work explicitly in relation to E.A.T.'s historical achievements while demonstrating how contemporary technology, social conditions, and artistic sensibilities have transformed the possibilities of artist-technology collaboration.

## Programmatic Focus Areas and Thematic Framework

### E.A.T.'s "Projects Outside Art" and Contemporary Thematic Relevance

The residency program explicitly centers on three thematic areas derived from E.A.T.'s 1969 "Projects Outside Art" initiative: urban ecology, connecting people through technology, and play as social practice[32][32]. These three themes merit individual examination, as they establish the conceptual parameters within which ZKM will evaluate resident projects and assess their relevance to the program's mission.

Urban ecology encompasses artistic engagement with environmental conditions, sustainability, ecosystem health, and the relationship between human communities and natural systems within urbanized contexts. Artists working within this thematic area might develop performance works that visualize urban environmental data, create participatory experiences promoting ecological awareness, design interactive systems revealing hidden ecological relationships within cities, or employ performance as a medium for environmental advocacy and activism. The inclusion of urban ecology reflects contemporary concerns regarding climate change, biodiversity loss, and the environmental challenges facing densely populated urban centers. Artistic engagement with ecological themes through performance and technology can make abstract environmental data viscerally comprehensible, mobilizing emotional and intellectual responses that motivate behavioral or political change.

Connecting people through technology addresses the paradox of contemporary technological development: despite unprecedented connectivity infrastructure, many individuals and communities experience profound isolation, fragmentation, and disconnection. This thematic area invites artistic exploration of how performance and technology can facilitate genuine human connection, community formation, and social cohesion. Artists might develop performance works that enable remote participation, create shared technological experiences fostering collective identity, design interactive systems promoting inter-generational dialogue, or employ technology-mediated performance to bridge geographically dispersed communities. The recognition of technology's potential for human connection directly addresses a central concern of Rauschenberg's collaborative practice: the use of technology as a mechanism for bringing diverse individuals together rather than isolating them.

Play as social practice represents the third thematic foundation, drawing from E.A.T.'s recognition that play constitutes a fundamental human activity with profound social, cognitive, and emotional significance. Artistic engagement with play might encompass performance works incorporating gaming elements, interactive systems inviting participant play and experimentation, or technology-enabled performances that foreground ludic elements and the pleasure of exploratory engagement. The inclusion of play as a legitimate artistic concern reflects recognition that artistic practice need not exclusively concern itself with weighty social or political themes; rather, lightness, humor, joy, and exploratory delight constitute significant artistic territories deserving cultivation and research.

### Digital Theatre and Formal Characteristics

The program explicitly positions itself as supporting "digital theatre," defined as a performance form characterized by the combination of performative art and the use of technology[3][3][3][3]. This definition deliberately avoids narrow technical specification in favor of conceptual articulation: digital theatre constitutes any performance work meaningfully integrating technological elements into its formal and conceptual structure. Importantly, the program emphasizes formal openness, explicitly welcoming "all forms of connection," including dance, performance, spoken or musical theatre, physical or object theatre, and mixed and hybrid formats[3][3][3][3][33]. This expansive formal inclusivity recognizes that performance art encompasses diverse disciplinary traditions, each of which can productively engage with technology in distinct ways.

Dance incorporating motion capture technology, live performers interacting with projected imagery, musicians performing with real-time algorithmic sound processing, actors in dialogue with artificial intelligence systems, physical performers navigating virtual reality environments, and countless other formal configurations all constitute legitimate digital theatre approaches. The program's formal openness suggests that ZKM seeks diverse artistic approaches rather than favoring particular technological or performative methodologies. Applicants should understand that their specific formal approach—whether grounded in contemporary dance, experimental theatre, live art, performance art, or other performance traditions—will not disadvantage their application; rather, coherent integration of technology with their particular formal tradition constitutes the critical evaluation criterion.

The program documentation emphasizes that applicants should draw inspiration from E.A.T.'s "Projects Outside Art" framework, suggesting that projects with explicit social, political, or community dimensions will receive favorable consideration[18][32][32]. ## The Past Residents: Understanding Institutional Continuities and Divergences

### Important Distinction: Archives Research Residency versus ZKM Rauschenberg Residencies

A critical distinction must be established for clarity: the 2026 ZKM Rauschenberg Residencies at the Hertzlab represent a newly inaugurated program launching specifically in 2026 to commemorate Rauschenberg's centennial[32][32]. Therefore, no previous cohorts of residents exist for the ZKM Hertzlab program specifically. However, the Robert Rauschenberg Foundation operates two distinct but related residency programs: the Archives Research Residency in New York and the Rauschenberg Residency in Captiva, Florida. Understanding these existing programs provides valuable insight into the Foundation's residency philosophy, the types of artists and scholars it supports, and the kinds of projects it values.

### The Robert Rauschenberg Foundation Archives Research Residency

The Robert Rauschenberg Foundation's Archives Research Residency operates independently from the ZKM partnership, providing one- to three-week residential periods during which scholars, artists, curators, and researchers conduct on-site research within the Foundation's archival collections housed at the Foundation's New York headquarters at 381 Lafayette Street[4][7][4][15][4][4][7]. The 2026 cohort of Archives Research Residents includes fourteen internationally diverse researchers: Nadja Abt (Vienna, Austria), Paul Bailey (London, UK), Lesley Loksi Chan (Hamilton, Canada), Alinka Echeverría (Mexico/London, UK), Nika Elder (Washington, DC), João Eduardo Peçanha de Freitas (São Paulo, Brazil), Evan Garza (North Adams, MA), Martha Jager (Amsterdam, The Netherlands), Erëmirë Krasniqi (Pristina, Kosovo), Roberta Minnucci (Rome, Italy), Carlos Prieto (Montreuil, France/Mexico City, Mexico), Paul Purgas (London, UK), Raymond Rohne (Hong Kong, China), and Raf Wollaert (Antwerp, Belgium)[6][12].

These residents exemplify the breadth of scholarly and artistic approaches the Foundation supports. Nadja Abt's project, "The Story of ROCI in the Former Centre for Art Exhibitions in the GDR, Berlin 1990," investigates Rauschenberg's Rauschenberg Overseas Culture Interchange (ROCI) Berlin exhibition and its cultural significance within the context of German reunification[12]. Abt, an artist, writer, and editor based in Berlin and Vienna, is currently serving as co-curator of "Rauschen," an exhibition examining the former Centre for Art Exhibitions in the German Democratic Republic (GDR) opening at ifa-Gallery Berlin in summer 2026[12]. Her research represents the type of historically grounded, geopolitically engaged scholarship the Foundation values.

Lesley Loksi Chan's project, "Moving Stoned Moon," traces the trajectory of Robert Rauschenberg's Stoned Moon Book as fragmentary intersections of art, science, and longing[12]. Through film, text, and archival investigation, Chan explores the book's incompletion as a living archive, weaving personal encounter with Stoned Moon materials into meditation on time, technology, and imagination[12]. Chan's project exemplifies how archival research can become personally transformative and artistically generative rather than remaining purely documentary.

Alinka Echeverría's project, "Mexico My Muse Chapter 5: Rauschenberg's ROCI, Potent Peaceful Powers, and Legacies of Intercultural Exchange," investigates underrepresented dimensions of Rauschenberg's engagement with Mexico through his ROCI project and his broader cultural collaborations with Mexican communities[12]. Echeverría's research will generate original art historical insights destined for wide publication, illuminating underexplored histories of intercultural exchange and artistic collaboration[12]. This project demonstrates the Archives Research Residency's commitment to supporting scholarship addressing previously overlooked or marginalized topics within Rauschenberg scholarship.

The diversity of these researchers—spanning geographic origins, disciplinary backgrounds, and research methodologies—demonstrates the Foundation's commitment to supporting heterogeneous approaches to Rauschenberg scholarship. Ages represented in the cohort range from emerging scholars through established researchers; geographic origins span six continents. This diversity reflects the Foundation's understanding that Rauschenberg's global artistic reach and collaborative philosophy attract scholars and artists from diverse backgrounds pursuing varied research objectives.

### The Rauschenberg Residency in Captiva, Florida

The Rauschenberg Residency in Captiva represents a distinct program initiated in 2012 at the site of Rauschenberg's former home and studio on Captiva Island, Florida[9][9][36]. This residency serves approximately 100 emerging and established artists and scholars annually through five-week residencies, providing time and space for research and artistic experimentation across multiple disciplines[9][9][36]. The Captiva residency operates as an invitation-based program focused on advancing new artistic work, extending practices into diverse media, and functioning as a research and development laboratory[9][9][36]. The 20-acre site spanning from the Gulf of Mexico to Pine Island Sound stimulates cross-disciplinary collaborations while fostering awareness of environmental conservation and local integration[9][9][36].

Recently, the Robert Rauschenberg Foundation announced that it had decided to sunset the Captiva residency program and sell the property following a multi-year study of the site's sustainability[36]. This decision reflects the Foundation's assessment that it could more effectively deploy resources supporting its mission through alternative programming structures. The decision is significant because it demonstrates the Foundation's willingness to evolve its programmatic offerings in response to changing institutional priorities and resource considerations. The ZKM Rauschenberg Residencies represent, in part, a strategic reallocation of Foundation resources toward international collaboration models that may reach broader artist populations than the geographically limited Captiva program.

### The Rauschenberg Centennial Award

In recognition of Rauschenberg's centennial, the Foundation established the Rauschenberg Centennial Award, providing one-time unrestricted grants of €100,000 to individual recipients across four disciplines: Art, Performance, Photography, and Writing[36]. Recipients included Senga Nengudi (Art), David Thomson (Performance), Chandra McCormick and Keith Calhoun (Photography), and Patricia Spears Jones (Writing)[36]. The award recognized excellence among artists and scholars who had participated in or been invited to participate in the Foundation's Captiva residency program, reflecting the Foundation's commitment to celebrating the ongoing achievements of its residency alumni and supporting artists across career stages and disciplinary traditions[36].

## Material, Media, and Project Type Expectations

### Technological Flexibility and Medium-Agnostic Approach

The program documentation emphasizes formal and technological openness, indicating that ZKM does not privilege particular technological platforms, software systems, or hardware configurations[3][3][3][3][33]. Rather than prescribing specific technical requirements, the program welcomes "technological openness (from Arduino to Lidar)," explicitly naming modest microcontroller platforms alongside sophisticated three-dimensional sensing technologies[33]. This formulation suggests that the program evaluates projects based on the appropriateness of technological choices to artistic objectives rather than on the sophistication or costliness of technology employed.

Arduino—an open-source microcontroller platform enabling creation of interactive physical computing systems—represents an accessible, inexpensive technology enabling artists to create responsive environments, interactive sculptures, and sensor-based systems. Lidar—light detection and ranging technology typically associated with autonomous vehicles and sophisticated spatial sensing—enables three-dimensional environmental mapping and sophisticated gesture recognition. The explicit inclusion of both technologies in the same formulation indicates that projects employing inexpensive, accessible technologies alongside projects requiring sophisticated technical infrastructure both warrant serious consideration.

The framework of technological flexibility reflects recognition that meaningful artistic-technological practice emerges across a spectrum of technical sophistication and cost considerations. A performance incorporating Arduino-based lighting control responding to dancer movement might generate more compelling artistic results than a project employing expensive projection mapping technology without rigorous conceptual grounding. ZKM's evaluation criteria reward artistic intelligence and conceptual clarity over technical complexity or expense.

### Expected Material and Resource Utilization

The €2,500 material cost allocation provides substantial resources for acquiring technologies, materials, and components essential to resident projects. This budget might support acquisition of specialized sensors, microcontrollers, projection equipment, audio interfaces, cables, construction materials, components for fabrication and prototyping, or rental of equipment unavailable for purchase. Applicants should note that this allocation represents the maximum available; specific projects might require substantially less, depending on the technological approach employed. Projects relying primarily on software and digital tools might require minimal material expenses, allowing reallocation of remaining funds toward other project needs or publication of research outcomes.

Projects in the media arts, performance art, and interactive installation domains typically incur material costs for: electronic components and hardware (sensors, controllers, displays, speakers); physical materials for construction (wood, metal, fabrics, structural materials); consumable supplies (solvents, adhesives, tape, fasteners); and potentially equipment rental when specialized technical systems exceed institutional inventory. Applicants should budget realistically for their specific technological approach while recognizing that the €2,500 allocation, while substantial, may not cover comprehensive equipment acquisition for technologically complex projects. Applicants should clarify whether materials fabricated during the residency become resident property or institutional property; this distinction may affect budgeting and project scope decisions.

### Project Type Specifications and Outcome Expectations

The program explicitly states that "the results may be presented in the form of prototypes, installations, participatory actions, or media interventions"[32][32]. This enumeration suggests multiple legitimate outcomes without implying that these categories exhaust the possibilities. Projects need not produce "finished" artworks in conventional senses; rather, prototypes, research installations, or experimental systems revealing insight into artistic-technological possibilities constitute legitimate residency outcomes. This flexibility acknowledges that three-month development periods may not permit production of complete, fully resolved artistic works; instead, substantial progress toward artistic goals, rigorous experimentation, and meaningful research outcomes constitute success.

The inclusion of "participatory actions" reflects recognition that contemporary performance art frequently incorporates audience participation and community engagement rather than maintaining boundaries between performers and spectators. Projects involving community engagement, audience participation, or collaborative creative processes fit within this category. "Media interventions" suggests projects addressing specific urban, social, or cultural contexts through temporary installations, performances, or interactive systems designed to provoke reflection, disruption, or dialogue. The public showcase on January 15, 2027, will provide opportunity for public presentation of residency outcomes, whatever formal or conceptual configuration the results assume.

## Practical Application Procedures and Submission Guidelines

### PDF Consolidation and Email Submission Process

The application process demands consolidation of all materials—portfolio documentation, project description, biographical information, and any supplementary materials—into a single PDF file submitted via email to image@zkm.de[5][5][33]. This requirement mandates that applicants possess either native PDF creation capabilities (through word processors, design software, or other applications) or knowledge of converting document formats into PDF. Applicants should ensure that their final PDF file maintains readable text, appropriately scaled images, and embedded multimedia links where necessary.

The single PDF requirement serves multiple practical functions: it eliminates the possibility of incomplete submissions wherein some application materials arrive while others remain lost in email transmission; it streamlines the evaluation process by consolidating all application materials in a single, consistently formatted package; and it respects the institution's preference for manageable digital document formats. Applicants should test their PDF files prior to submission to confirm that all materials display correctly, that embedded links function properly, and that file sizes remain within reasonable email transmission parameters (typically under 25 megabytes for most email systems, though smaller files are preferable).

### Portfolio Organization and Documentation Standards

Portfolio materials should be organized clearly, with descriptive captions identifying each included work, its creation date, technologies employed, performance context, and duration. High-quality documentation is essential, particularly for video materials comprising performance records. Video files should be sufficiently compressed for file transfer while maintaining image quality adequate for evaluating the artistic work. Where physical installations or interactive systems constitute previous work, photographic documentation supplemented by explanatory text describing the work's interactive properties, technological components, and audience engagement modes will communicate the work effectively.

Documentation conventions adopted should follow professional standards typical in the contemporary art world. Applicants should consider how their portfolio presents their artistic trajectory and demonstrates growing sophistication, technical competence, and conceptual development. The portfolio should make clear that the applicant possesses the skills and experience necessary to undertake a three-month intensive residency project resulting in a publicly presented outcome.

### Project Description Strategic Considerations

The two-page project description limitation demands strategic choices regarding what information receives priority emphasis. Applicants must communicate their artistic concept, technological approach, implementation schedule, and budget considerations within this constrained space. Effective applications will likely employ clear section headings, concise paragraphing, and strategic use of white space to enhance readability within the page constraint. The project description should assume that evaluators possess general artistic literacy but may not possess deep familiarity with specific technical domains; consequently, technical terminology should be explained clearly without excessive jargon.

Strategic applicants should devote the primary emphasis to conceptual clarity and artistic vision, with technological and budgetary information supporting rather than dominating the narrative. The project description should answer critical questions: What artistic problem or question does this project investigate? Why is this particular technology essential to addressing this problem? How will the three-month residency timeline enable meaningful progress toward the project's objectives? What will the public outcome constitute, and why should audiences care about this work?

## Conclusion: Synthesis of Opportunities and Strategic Recommendations

The ZKM Rauschenberg Residencies 2026 represent a substantial and timely opportunity for artists working at the intersection of performance art and creative technology to engage in intensive three-month artistic development supported by world-class technical infrastructure, institutional mentoring, and connection to historical precedent through the E.A.T. archive[1][3][1][32][32]. The program's timing—coinciding with Robert Rauschenberg's centennial and explicitly grounded in the historical legacy of Experiments in Art and Technology—positions selected residents within a meaningful lineage of artist-engineer collaboration while enabling them to advance this legacy into contemporary contexts addressing urgent social and ecological concerns[1][3][32][32].

The comprehensive support provided—€4,000 scholarship, €1,000 travel subsidy, €2,500 material allocation, housing, workspace access, technical infrastructure, mentoring, and archival access—demonstrates genuine institutional commitment to resident artistic development[1][3][3][3][18][3][3]. The program's openness regarding technological and formal approaches, coupled with its explicit disinterest in technological trendiness, creates space for diverse artistic methodologies and conceptual frameworks. Artists working with modest or unconventional technologies, artists from geographic locations distant from Western European artistic centers, and artists addressing underrepresented topics or communities will likely find the program welcoming to their proposals.

The April 12, 2026 deadline, now only twelve days distant as of March 30, 2026, creates urgent impetus for immediate action by interested artists. Any artist matching the program's eligibility criteria—working at the intersection of performance and technology, possessing valid health insurance for Germany, demonstrating professional artistic practice through portfolio documentation, and able to articulate a compelling three-month project—should prioritize application preparation. The program's limited selection of three residents from potentially extensive applications demands that submissions demonstrate exceptional artistic vision, rigorous conceptual grounding, realistic planning, and clear alignment with institutional priorities. Applications employing this report's strategic recommendations—compelling conceptual frameworks, coherent integration of technology serving artistic objectives, engagement with the three thematic areas, awareness of E.A.T. historical precedents, and professional presentation—will present the strongest competitive possibilities.

The ZKM Rauschenberg Residencies represent not merely a funding opportunity but rather an invitation to participate in the ongoing legacy of artistic innovation, interdisciplinary collaboration, and socially engaged art practice that Rauschenberg pioneered. Selected residents will work within the physical spaces and conceptual frameworks that Rauschenberg and E.A.T. established while extending these principles into contemporary artistic, technological, social, and ecological challenges. This represents a rare and substantial opportunity for artistic development, institutional validation, and positioning within a global artistic network committed to advancing collaborative art-technology practice toward social transformation.
